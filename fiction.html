Lessons in Chemistry by Bonnie Garmus (2022)

I loved this book! Elizabeth Zott was not your average 1960's woman. She was a brilliant chemist who was acutely aware of and intolerant of the rampant sexism in her workplace but did not let that stop her from working toward her goals. It was fascinating but also infuriating being engrossed in her perspective and feeling what she went through as a woman during that time period. She dared other women to change the status quo. The book also made me reflect on the treatment of women today in American society and draw parallels between then and now. I highly recommed this book. Bonus: AppleTV also made it into a show which I recently started. (Trigger warning: Both the book and show include scenes of sexual assault.)

The Midnight Library by Matt Haig (2020)

This book had a very interesting premise. Between life and death there is a library. When Nora finds herself in the Midnight Library, she has a chance to make things right. Up until this point, her life has been full of unhappiness and regret. But things are about to change. The books in the Midnight Library enable Nora to live as if she had done things differently, undoing each regret and attempting to work out her perfect life. But things aren't always what she imagined they'd be, and soon she is working against the clock. Before time runs out, she must answer the ultimate question: what is the best way to live? I saw so many great things about this book but rated it like I did simply because I felt it was a little slow and didn't live up to the hype in my opinion.

The Lion Women of Tehran by Marjan Kamali (2024)

This was a heartfelt novel of friendship, betrayal, and redemption set against three transformative decades in Tehran, Iran. I had never read a book set in Iran and it was fascinating to learn what living there as a woman was like in the 1950's amid political turmoil. Although this is fiction, the author spent some of her childhood in Iran during a pivitol time in its history and drew on her own experiences with a friendship she had growing up. I loved this book because of the beautiful descriptions of the Iranian setting, foods, and lifestyle in the book. It was a moving story and extremely well-written with characters I came to love!

Malibu Rising by Taylor Jenkins Reid (2021)

This was an exciting, quick read that transports you to the lives of 4 famous celebrity siblings. They throw an epic party to celebrate the end of the summer. But over the course of 24 hours, their lives change dramatically. This book was a purely "for fun" read, and it truly was a fun read imagining the lives of celebrities!

Eleanor Oliphant is Completely Fine by Gail Honeyman (2017)

Meet Eleanor Oliphant: She is socially awkward and says out loud exactly what she is thinking. Her life has been the same for decades, mundane office work and then solitary weekends spent only with frozen pizza, vodka, and bitter phone chats with Mummy. But everything changes when Eleanor meets Raymond, the unattractive, unhygienic IT guy from her office. They rescue one another from the lives of isolation and Raymond helps heal her deep emotional wounds from her past. I thought this story was simultaneously strange and charming. The author enables you to really feel what Eleanor felt and see from her unique perspective. This book helped me remember that everyone is dealing with their own internal struggles and past experiences that makes them who they are.

Pineapple Street by Jenny Jackson (2023)

Step into the shoes of a wealthy, well-connected Brooklyn Heights family. Darley, the oldest daughter, followed her heart, trading her career and her inheritance for motherhood; Sasha, a middle-class girl, married into the family and finds herself cast as an outsider; and Georgiana, the youngest, has fallen in love with someone she can’t have and must decide what kind of person she wants to be. Pineapple Street is an enjoyable, escapist novel about the peculiar unknowability of someone else’s family, the miles between the haves and have-nots, and the craziness of family and love.

The Secret Life of Bees by Sue Monk Kidd (2001)

Set in South Carolina in 1964, this book tells the story of Lily Owens, who escapes her abusive father along with her fierce-hearted black "stand-in mother," Rosaleen. They are taken in by an eccentric trio of black beekeeping sisters. I liked the female power and resistance presented in this story. It wasn't my top favorite book of the year but it was fun to read and then watch the movie after.

It Ends with Us/It Starts with Us by Colleen Hoover (2016/2022)

I know there is a lot of controversy with the movie and the Blake/Justin drama but these books were so good in my opinion. They were emotional, deep, and page turners that always left me wanting to read more. I loved how the relationships developed and seeing Lily, the main character, take steps to get out of her abusive relationship and find something better for her and her daughter. For the record, I did love the movie too and can't wait for the next one.

The Alchemist by Paulo Coelho

I found this book at a thrift store and thought the cover was really beautiful. I had heard of this book and knew it was a classic so I gave it a shot. I was actually pretty bored of it and kept thinking it would get better. Yes, I think there were some little lessons in it but the story just wasn't captivating to me which is why I rated it low. Not a deep, meaningful review I realize but oh well.

A Court of Thorns and Roses, 1 by Sarah J. Maas (2015)

This was my first shot at fantasy romance. And I could not get past the faeries with wings and their pointy ears. I honestly tried. I even tried reading the second book as well. It is so incredibly popular and I was told so many times to read this book and to push through it. But I actually am pretty sensitive to violence and gore and there was a little too much descriptive violence in it to the point where it gave me bad dreams. I'm a sensitive little muffin. So I don't think I can read ACOTAR further. I do agree, however, that the story overall is quite good, complex, and deep so I understand the hype...(but why must it have fairies?)
