Solito: A Memior by Javier Zamora (2022)

A memoir as gripping as it is moving, Solito provides an immediate and intimate account not only of the treacherous and near-impossible 3,000 mile journey to get across the U.S border from Central America, but also of the miraculous kindness and love delivered at the most unexpected moments. Solito is Javier Zamora’s story, but it’s also the story of millions of others who had no choice but to leave home in search of a better life. This book was very descriptive and through a 9 year-olds eyes. It was an emotional and incredible read.

Tattoos on the Heart: The Power of Boundless Compassion by Gregory Boyle (2010)

This book changed the way I think about people and helped me see the good in humanity in a population I admittedly hadn't previously given much thought to, gangs in LA. Father Gregory teaches through parables about individuals he met that no matter where people live or what their circumstances may be, everyone needs boundless, restorative love. Gorgeous and uplifting, this book shows the impact unconditional love can have on your life and reminds us that no life is less valuable than another.

Cultish: The Language of Fanaticism by Amanda Montell (2021)

These days demonstrations of extremism is not hard to notice among groups of people. The psychology of cults kind of fascinates me. The author analyzes the social science of cult influence and discusses answers to questions like "What makes “cults” so intriguing and frightening? What makes them powerful? Could it happen to someone I know? Or to me?" It's interesting to pause and reflect on cultish influence in my own life or things I've observed in my circles-- even just cultish language, which actually is the key to manufacturing intense ideology, community, and us/them attitudes. I rated it 4 stars just because there were some parts that weren't too engaging for me personally, but really I thought this was very informative and interesting.

I'm cheating putting this on here because I am only a few chapters in but wowowow! I already am rating this 5/5 stars because I have learned so much! I'm excited to apply this to my life. More Than a Body: Your Body is an Instrument, Not an Ornament by Lindsay Kite, PhD & Lexie Kite, PhD (2020)

Positive body image isn’t believing your body looks good; it is knowing your body is good, regardless of how it looks. The authors present an action plan that arms you with the skills you need to reconnect with your whole self and free yourself from the constraints of self-objectification. I thought the message of this book was incredible and needed but I just had a hard time getting through it because a bit long and dry. Maybe I just read it at the wrong time in life. Regardless, I would recommend this book because the message needs to be downloaded into ALL of our brains!

Ejaculate Responsibly: A Whole New Way to Think About Abortion by Gabrielle Blair (2022)

Hey, did you notice what I did there?? 6/5 stars!!! Yes, I can do that because this is my own website. Everyone needs to hear the message of this book! And guess what, they CAN! Because this is a 3 hour audio book (and even faster at 1.5x speed). In a series of 28 brief arguments, the author deftly makes the case for moving the abortion debate away from controlling and legislating women’s bodies and instead directs the focus on men’s lack of accountability in preventing unwanted pregnancies. It's not a boring series of essays either. Blair builds her argument in an engaging and enjoyable way.

She walks readers through the basics of fertility (men are 50x more fertile than woman), the unfair burden placed on women when it comes to preventing pregnancy (90% of the birth control market is for women), the wrongheaded stigmas around birth control for men (condoms make sex less pleasurable, vasectomies are scary and emasculating), and the counterintuitive reality that men, who are fertile 100% of the time, take little to no responsibility for preventing pregnancy. The result is a compelling and convincing case for placing the responsibility—and burden—of preventing unwanted pregnancies away from women and onto men. Absolutely incredible, fact-driven book that our society needs to consider for the health of women.

This one intrigued me because the author technically grew up Mormon (like I did), however her family believed a super fundamentalist, survivalist, extreme, fringe version of Mormonism in rural Idaho. This is the story of Tara and how she educates herself and how that education gave her the perspective to see her life through new eyes and granted her the will to change it.

Invisible Women: Data Bias in a World Designed for Men by Caroline Criado Perez (2019)

I haven't quite finished this one yet but I will soon. It is shocking/appaling/interesting to see how much data fails to take into account gender, because it treats men as the default and women as atypical. Because of this, bias and discrimination are baked into society. And women pay huge costs for this bias, in time, money, and often with their lives. It's fascinating to see how things I've felt in my life as a female are consistent with real statistics. I didn't give it a full 5 stars because it can just be a bit data and stats heavy (understandably) which can be a bit dry at times. But the sentiment of the book is fascinating.

The Montessori Toddler: A Parent's Guide to Raising a Curious and Responsible Human Being by Simone Davies (2019)

As a parent, this was a great read. Montessori is an alternative education system where, instead of the teacher standing at the front of the classroom telling the kids what they need to know, the children are free to explore a classroom of well-designed materials. This allows kids to follow their unique natural rhythm and development and make discoveries for themselves. This book teaches how to make your home more Montessori aligned, with any sized home and budget. I appreciated that aspect of it and found it encouraging, applicable, and attainable as a parent who isn't trained in Montessori methods. Loved this book! The authors have also written a baby-focused book too which I haven't read yet but plan to.

Faith After Doubt: Why Your Beliefs Stopped Working and What to Do About It by Brian D. McLaren (2021)

This book was interesting to me and applicable to my life as I fairly recently left the religion I grew up in. The author, a former pastor, proposes a 4 stage model of faith development in which questions and doubt are not the enemy of faith, but rather offer a path forward that can help sincere and thoughtful people leave behind unnecessary baggage and intensify their commitment to what they value most. I read this one a while ago so I can't remember specifics on why I gave this rating, but I think some of the philisophical parts were a little too much for my brain at 6 AM on the elliptical. However, I do remember finding it quite insightful overall!

Good Inside: A Guide to Becoming the Parent You Want to Be by Dr. Becky Kennedy (2022)

I think I need to listen to this book annually because in the throws of parenting (particuarly those hard moments) I often forget the amazing lessons from this book. Dr. Becky’s empowering and effective approach is a model that prioritizes *connecting* with our kids over correcting them. She teaches that all kids are good and when we recognize that we can parent them better. I love this book and Dr. Becky's instagram too @drbeckyatgoodinside.

No-Drama Discipline by Daniel J Seigel, MD & Tina Payne Bryson, PhD (2016)

Another incredible parenting book! My first parenting book actually and one that was recommended by a therapist. This NYT Bestseller highlights the fascinating link between a child’s neurological development and the way a parent reacts to misbehavior. This book provides an effective, compassionate road map for dealing with tantrums, tensions, and tears—without causing a scene. I love this book and it truly serves as a guide in my parenting journey!

Why We Sleep: Unlocking the Power of Sleep and Dreams by Matthew Walker, PhD (2017)

This is a book that was a bit boring and long but very fascinating at the same time. I still think about it years later at random times. Whenever I stay up late, like right now as I'm writing this, I think about my health and how sleep truly, seriously is so important. I used to think sleep was just something you had to do so you weren't tired the next day. But there is so much more to it. This book is full of scientific studies on the benefits of sleep and the physical and mental consequences of not getting enough sleep, and the right kinds of sleep. While I don't remember every fact and statistic from this data-heavy book, I do remember the big takeaways including that sleep is crucial to our wellbeing and even has effects on society as a whole. Really great read if you are a person! Because if you're a person, that means you sleep. :-P

Does literature get better than this!? Successfully teaching you how to get your child potty-trained in one weekend? I think not! This book was clear, quick, and straight forward...plus it is tried and true. It's a yes from me, dawg.
